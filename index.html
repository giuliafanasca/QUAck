// The Problem

Location-aware AI is still guessing.

Ambient AI platforms — glasses, spatial computers, wearables — are proliferating fast. They know where you are. But they operate on generic knowledge: training data, public APIs, assumptions.

The space itself has no voice. The AI decides what to surface, when, and why — with zero input from the entity that owns and understands that space. The result is a lowest-common-denominator experience that serves no one well.

And from the user's side: you don't know what you can ask. You don't know what's available. You navigate blind.

// The Gap

Knowing vs. being told.

There's a fundamental difference between an AI that knows about the Colosseum from training data — and the Colosseum actively declaring what it wants to offer you, right now, in this context.

The first is a search engine pulling from a generic corpus. The second is a curated experience controlled by the entity that owns the space.

// The Proposal

A behavioral label protocol for physical space. A companion that wears it.

QUAck proposes two interlocked layers:

The Protocol. An open format — like robots.txt or ActivityPub — where any physical location can host its own endpoint. That endpoint is a behavioral label: it declares the space's identity, the capabilities it wants to expose, the constraints it wants to impose, and the tone it wants the AI to adopt. Each location controls its own data.

The Companion. A persistent AR duck — always present in your field of view, always yours. It reads behavioral labels on proximity and surfaces the space's declared capabilities through natural conversation, hands-free. Not a notification. Not an app. And when it enters a new space, it dresses for the occasion.

Technically: QUAck endpoints are lightweight JSON APIs discoverable via GPS geofencing, with NFC and QR code as physical fallbacks. Each location controls its own endpoint — letting venues expose official capabilities, real-time services, and branded conversational behavior directly to the user's companion.

// The Experience

What the user lives.

The companion is a duck — an AR avatar that lives persistently in your field of view. Small, recognizable, yours. As you move through the world, it reads the spaces around you and adapts. The adaptation is visible: it changes costume.

1
You enter a space. The duck gets dressed.
The app detects the location's behavioral label endpoint. The duck updates visually — a tiny hard hat, a museum badge, a store tag — a costume accessory that signals the active context. You see it happen. You know where you are in the spatial web. No notification. No UI. Just a duck in a new outfit.
2
The space speaks. Through the duck.
The duck introduces the space — in the tone, language, and personality defined by the behavioral label. At the Colosseum it's measured and historical. Inside a concept store it's editorial and precise. It's the space talking to you, through a character you trust.
3
You interact, hands-free
You ask questions. You request functions. The companion accesses only what the space has declared available — no guessing, no hallucinating, no going outside the curated perimeter. The conversation is the interface.
4
You leave. The duck undresses.
Cross the threshold and the costume drops. The duck is just your duck again. The identity was always yours — the context was always theirs. The same character, every space, no confusion about who it's working for.
// The Paradigm

Not a new app. The infrastructure for a spatial internet.

Nobody "manages" the web. Every site is responsible for its own content. The browser just renders it. QUAck applies the same logic to physical space — each location owns and hosts its own endpoint, the app just reads it.
